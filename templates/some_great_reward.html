Some Great Reward (1984)
Released in 1984, Some Great Reward marked a major step forward for Depeche Mode, both musically and lyrically. The album introduced a deeper industrial sound, blending metallic percussion with emotional themes of love, faith, and morality. Tracks like “People Are People” brought the band mainstream success while others, such as “Blasphemous Rumours” and “Master and Servant,” showcased their willingness to challenge social and religious norms. Some Great Reward cemented Depeche Mode’s reputation as innovators of electronic music with substance and edge.
Songs from Some Great Reward in order
  • Something to Do
    A dark, energetic opener that blends mechanical beats with themes of boredom, desire, and restless obsession, setting the tone for the album’s edgy, provocative mood.
  • Lie to Me
    A moody, synth-driven track that explores mistrust and emotional manipulation, balancing vulnerability with a haunting sense of resignation.
  • People Are People
    An upbeat yet politically charged song that calls for empathy and understanding, criticizing prejudice and highlighting the irrationality of human conflict.
  • It Doesn't Matter
    A gentle, melancholic track about unrequited love and emotional detachment, combining tender lyrics with a minimalist electronic backdrop.
  • Stories of Old
    A reflective and slightly cynical song about the fading idealism of love, contrasting romantic expectations with the realities of human relationships.
  • Somebody
    A heartfelt piano ballad expressing a longing for genuine connection and unconditional love, standing out as one of the band’s most intimate and emotional songs.
  • Master and Servant
    A bold, provocative track that uses themes of dominance and submission as a metaphor for social and personal power dynamics, wrapped in a catchy industrial dance beat.
  • If You Want
    An energetic, upbeat song about taking chances and embracing desire, offering a rare sense of optimism amid the album’s darker themes.
  • Blasphemous Rumours
    A haunting and controversial track that questions faith and the existence of divine justice, telling tragic stories that highlight the randomness of life and death.
